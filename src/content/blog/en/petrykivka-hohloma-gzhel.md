When people see Petrykivka painting for the first time, they often say: "That looks a bit like Khokhloma" or "It reminds me of Gzhel, just with more colors." These comparisons are understandable — all three traditions use decorative painting and floral motifs. But that's roughly where the similarities end.

Petrykivka painting, Khokhloma, and Gzhel emerged under different historical conditions, served different purposes, and developed their own distinct artistic principles. Let's look at what actually separates them.

## Origins: from the peasant house to the craft workshop

The most fundamental difference between these traditions lies in where they came from.

Petrykivka painting was born in the village of Petrykivka in the Dnipropetrovsk region, roughly in the 18th century. It began as wall painting inside village houses. Women decorated walls, stoves, windows, ceiling beams, and shelves in their own homes, turning everyday space into something festive. Each composition was created without a preliminary sketch and existed only until the next whitewashing of the house washed it away.

That's why Petrykivka painting was, from the start, an art of everyday life rather than an art made for sale.

Khokhloma has a very different history. It emerged around the 17th century in what is now the Nizhny Novgorod region as a craft for decorating wooden tableware. Artisans made spoons, bowls, cups, mugs, and furniture, which were sold at fairs. From the very beginning, Khokhloma was tied to the production of decorative objects.

Gzhel, in turn, developed as a center of ceramic production near Moscow. It produced clay pottery, tiles, apothecary vessels, and later porcelain. Painting was only the final stage in making the finished object.

So while Petrykivka began as an art of decorating the home, Khokhloma and Gzhel began as craft industries.

## Material shapes the art

Another fundamental difference lies in what surface the painting is done on.

Petrykivka painting isn't tied to any single material. Historically it covered the walls of houses, but today artists work on paper, canvas, wood, furniture, musical instruments, glass, and even modern interior objects. The style itself exists independently of the surface.

Khokhloma is the opposite. It's almost impossible to imagine without wood. Before painting, an object goes through a complex technological process: it's turned on a lathe, primed, coated with special compounds, then painted and fired. This very process is what creates Khokhloma's famous golden effect.

Gzhel is even more tightly bound to its material. It exists only on ceramics or porcelain, since its signature blue design is created with cobalt paint that only reveals its deep color after high-temperature firing.

## Color as an artistic language

Even someone unfamiliar with folk art can easily tell these three styles apart just by their colors.

Petrykivka painting is a true celebration of color. Artists use reds, yellows, greens, blues, pinks, oranges, and purples, aiming to capture the richness of the living natural world.

Khokhloma, by contrast, relies on a limited palette. Its core colors are black, gold, and red, sometimes complemented by green. That contrast is what gives it its solemn, decorative character.

Gzhel has the most restrained color system of the three. Its world consists almost entirely of white porcelain and blue cobalt. Instead of using many colors, the artist works with dozens of shades of a single blue to create volume and depth.

## Nature as a source of inspiration

All three traditions draw on the plant world, but each does so differently.

Petrykivka painting grew out of the nature of the Ukrainian steppe. You'll find viburnum (kalyna), grapes, hollyhocks, asters, daisies, and wheat ears in it. At the same time, the artist almost never copies a plant literally — they create its artistic image: generalized, imaginative, yet very much alive.

In Khokhloma, plant motifs are more conventional. Most often these are rowan or wild strawberries, leaves, and decorative curls, subordinated to an ornamental rhythm and repetition.

In Gzhel, flowers also play an important role, but they primarily serve to decoratively fill the surface of the porcelain.

## Composition: what "grows" and what repeats

The difference becomes especially clear when you look closely at composition.

Petrykivka painting seems to grow before your eyes. Flowers, leaves, berries, and branches naturally flow into one another. The composition unfolds freely, like a plant opening toward the sun — which is why no two works, even by the same master, are ever exactly alike.

In Khokhloma, rhythm is what matters most. The ornament repeats, forming a decorative pattern that evenly wraps around the surface of the object.

Gzhel leans toward symmetry and balance. Its composition is often built around a central flower, complemented by decorative elements around the edges.

## The art of a single brushstroke

One of the most valuable features of Petrykivka painting is its technique.

The master doesn't sketch the outline of a flower first. Instead, the form is born in a single movement of the brush. This is how Petrykivka's famous strokes are created — "zernyatko" (little grain), "hrebinets" (comb), "horishok" (little nut), the "transitional stroke," and many others.

In Khokhloma, the brushstroke is more subordinated to the decorative pattern. Forms tend to have sharper boundaries and repeat from object to object.

In Gzhel, the core mastery lies in working with a single color, creating smooth transitions from nearly transparent light blue to deep, saturated dark blue.

## Symbolism

Petrykivka painting carries deep symbolic meaning rooted in Ukrainian folk culture. The viburnum symbolizes family and the continuity of generations, birds represent happiness and prosperity, the tree of life connects past, present, and future, flowers stand for beauty and vitality, and berries symbolize abundance and fertility.

In Khokhloma and Gzhel, this symbolic layer is much less pronounced. The emphasis there falls not on the meaning of individual images but on the decorativeness and harmony of the ornament itself.

## Artistic philosophy

Perhaps the most important difference between these traditions isn't about color or technique at all — it's about a way of thinking.

Petrykivka painting is an art of living nature. It's built on improvisation, creative freedom, and each master's individual hand. A composition isn't copied from a template — it's born in the process of working.

Khokhloma is an art of rhythm and decorative ornament. Its beauty lies in repetition, consistency, and the refined finishing of an object.

Gzhel strives for refinement and purity. White porcelain and blue cobalt create a restrained, almost classical aesthetic, where the nobility of the material itself plays the leading role.

## Why is Petrykivka painting unique?

Despite superficial similarities to other forms of decorative art, Petrykivka painting holds a special place in world cultural heritage.

In 2013, it was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. Importantly, what earned this international recognition wasn't individual objects or ornaments, but a living tradition — the knowledge, techniques, teaching system, and creative way of thinking passed down from generation to generation.

It's precisely this living nature of the tradition that makes Petrykivka painting unique. It's an art form with no two identical works, since every composition is born anew — staying connected to a centuries-old tradition while still reflecting the personality of its author.

That's the core difference between Petrykivka painting and Khokhloma or Gzhel: it doesn't just decorate objects — it preserves the living artistic language of Ukrainian culture, one that continues to evolve today.
